KOIOS - what is it?

"Koios is an open learning infrastructure. A place to learn, teach and connect while earning rewards."

Initiated by The Hague University of Applied Sciences, this learning platform was created to serve educational content for students in the minor program "Introduction to Blockchain" with course sizes of around Ã˜ 300 students. On the long run however, it is planned to expand over an entire network of "Hogescholen" (Universities of Applied Sciences) in The Netherlands. Currently, it is home to 5 minor courses.

As a UX Designer I was integral part within the process of relaunching the platform. During that process, supported the team with words and deeds, the creation of user stories, extensive user testing, designing the new platform's interfaces and took effort in shaping processes within the Tech team to a higher UX maturity level.

Changing the state of education for university students

My team

During the process I was not alone - the team behind KOIOS consists of lecturers, lawyers, Blockchain experts, content creators and a dedicated tech team for the platform itself. While I was part of a diverse tech team, my core role was basically the gateway between developers and purely visual designers / content creators.

Putting everyone on the same page

When I joined the team, there were only two employees. While using offline tools might have worked out in this constellation, we decided to move all our assets to a more collaborative environment. As this was my first time working collaboratively on Figma as well, this came with a strong learning curve. Later I will explain, how together with the development team, we managed go one step further and fully integrate Figma the development process.

Process insights

Iterations

As befits a clean UX process, we first looked at the needs of our users and started prototyping on paper / whiteboard in very small, iterative pieces. This enabled us to be flexible in the process for changes and the prioritisation of features.

Collaborative Heuristic Evaluation

Before letting the users check out the prototype, it is always clever to test the overall functionality and assessing the service based on a clear framework of heuristics .

At KOIOS, we used the 10 heurisics from the Nielsen Norman group to make a consistent analysis of the status quo to iron out the biggest problems before user testing and incorporate multiple perspectives from the entire tech team.

https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics/

In order to make the topic easy to understand for everyone in the team, I built a framework that presents each heuristic aspect in detail and makes it tangible for everyone so that they can easily evaluate and fill it in.

Remote User Testing

Although we were also able to do some user testing in person, Covid and decentralised teams made this increasingly difficult. Without being in the same room and being able to drink a coffee and thus create a consistent testing environment, it was a real challenge.

To make this possible in a remote environment, I also developed a framework that ensures a clean flow of remote testing sessions.

The sessions were initially conducted via zoom, as it is theoretically also possible to share the smartphone screen. However, this turned out to be too complicated for many participants, why we changed to Microsoft Teams and prompted them to show their screen through the camera.

The sessions were very exciting in terms of individual user experiences and problems of individual users with special devices. Although we designed from the beginning for the smallest possible screen (iPhone SE), we found problems with Android devices, for example, whose browser bar took up a lot of space. One consequence of this was an easy to activate full-screen mode.

Further improvements in collaboration

Airtable as a project management tool might be great and flexible for project managers.

Figma is a great tool for designers. But where can we quickly gather ideas and think ahead? Without sitting at the same table? In the process, great ideas often got lost in the Airtable and were never seen again.

One of the consequences we drew from this was that we started using Miro as a collaborative software for idea collection, flow charts - and ultimately even for low fidelity prototypes. In principle all this is also possible with Figma - but what's the problem here? As a team, we realised that we needed something that everyone in the meeting could quickly understand. Miro proved to be a super efficient tool and took our workflow to the next level - especially in unrestrained ideation phases.

A new direction

As part of an ever-changing start-up, old structures may be overturned and new paths may be taken that no one has yet explored.

Team and processes

Since we are developing on a whole platform in a team of only four people and are not available to you for a whole week to work on it, we attached great importance to a smooth handoff process right from the start. The classic linear process seemed a bit rigid and left little room for small iterations.

Independence from Webflow

As a decentralised educational platform, we also wanted to free ourselves from dependencies and external parties. Webflow was fine for a small team with a visual designer and developer. But as soon as tailor-made user experiences come to play, usually extensive frameworks and huge teams are usually required.

Figma API for a fast & streamlined development process

So our lead developer came up with the idea to use the Figma API to feed the platform itself. This suddenly allowed us to design and see a result only moments later.

There was only one catch: This requires a lot of adjustments and not always all elements are displayed properly.

So we had to work closely together in this respect to make the converter somehow working.

Custom Tags

For some elements there are certain tags required, that trigger certain behaviour in the JavaScript that is assembling everything

The workspace

When production is dependant on the structure of your design, you end up designing for both the user and the production.

The difference to UX projects in the past

From previous projects I was used to delivering finished screens, after which the project was finished for me. I had no influence on what happened to the prototypes. Although I have always worked with components and clean layer structures, I didn't have to think responsively or prepare the auto-layouts that would work as CSS Flex-box in the live version.

Suddenly, I had to structure the prototype for a smooth development process

Due to the fact that it was suddenly necessary to structure prototypes correctly and tag them, working in these processes gave me a completely different feeling for structuring prototypes, their components and variants. Although other projects do not yet work according to this process, it helped me on a completely different level: The organisation and multiple use of design assets and building responsive prototypes for different screen sizes.

High-fidelity is not yet - but that's not so bad either.

Doesn't look high fidelity to you? There are two reasons!

With using a converter tool, some highly polished layouts using shadows and such are yet too complicated to implement.

On the other hand, this carefully simplified, somewhat nerdy design corresponds exactly to the target group we want to address. Just think of dev.to or hackernoon.com.

On the other hand, the goal should of course be to adapt the tool in the long term in such a way that designers can live out their high-fidelity designs so that the live version really looks 1:1 to what was designed in Figma.

The reason that we need to make the design work for the converting tool is why currently we are keeping the design as simple as possible.

Quick iterations: Dark Mode

One of the main characteristics of our target group (students, traders, people interested in finance, techies) is working at night. As our screens in Figma were all designed in light mode and a second version would have required a lot of effort just for dark mode, a CSS filter was the tool of choice. With only a few lines of code we could save ourselves a lot of work. The only further adjustments we had to do were telling every color-neutral element to maintain it's color (or: not to be inverted)
