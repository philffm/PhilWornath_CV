A brief introduction

My journey in User Experience Design already started way before I decided to start studying User Experience Design.

In my position as an Online Marketing Manager in the SEO and SEA field, the digital user journey always played a crucial role, which was the reason why I was focussing on digital analytics and landing page optimisation. After attending several meetups with UX and product focus in my free time, I started seeing UX everywhere - both in the digital and physical world. Both products for consumers and industrial use cases.

Suddenly it was not anymore about landing pages, which is the main reason why I decided to leave the Online Marketing field and discover how to apply User Experience in the real world, with a higher sense of purpose than solely focussing on high conversions or how to persuade people to buy more products.

What really got me excited in this course

Creating products with a clear purpose that follow thorough research and testing and can include touch points with non-screen interaction is one major reason why I chose to study User Experience Design instead of UI Design in particular. For me, creating the most beautiful, artsy user interfaces was never the focus, but rather getting a holistic overview about all the touch points, different target audiences run through when making use of the product, asking critical questions and validate assumptions by conducting user interviews and testing, while shaping the product in an iterative way.

When it comes to creating physical products, it adds just yet another layer for me: The possibilities in creating morphed experiences, where physical "things" interact with the user, while at the same time communicate with servers all around the world are enormous.

I am lucky to have chosen The Hague University to study UX Design, as "shaping" products comes in two dimensions here: Both in the digital and physical realm.

This holistic overview I was able to gain during my studies at The Hague University, was mostly thanks to a broad-ranging selection of courses, that not only include Psychology and Interaction Design, but also a Programming Bootcamp and Human Technologies, which focusses more on means of interaction without typical screen interface. This course was designed to make us tinker and create with components, that can capture and react to human behaviour.

During this course, I developed a deep connection to hardware prototyping. Although I always have been IoT-affine and scripted Home Automations on Raspberry Pi based server involving Zigbee and other protocols, Writing code and giving this code to a "thing" that within seconds shows according behaviour is just as magical as creating life.

Challenge 2: Wearables

When I first used my smartwatch in 2015, a Moto 360 the benefit was clear: It is an extension to my phone. I was hoping to use it primarily for bicycle navigation back then but it never really worked out, because the watch was not always in sight during the ride and it seemed even easier to get the phone out of my pocket. I concluded that watches in general might be just the tip of the iceberg in wearables we might see in the future and Interaction in motion should be highly considered when designing them.
